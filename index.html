Childhood Lead Poisoning in Wisconsin

Lead poisoning can happen to someone of any age, but has the most devastating life long consequences when it happens in early childhood. There are two main sources of childhood lead poisoning: lead paint and lead plumbing. Both were standard before 1950 and lead paint was standard until 1979. Lead paint causes many more cases of poisoning than lead plumbing and typically causes even more as houses age and paint flakes. The threshold for lead poisoning used to be set at a blood lead level (BLL) of 10 mcg/dL, but recently has been lowered to 5 mcg/dL as it was found that even lower levels caused long term health effects.
The percentage of older housing differs markedly from one county to another in Wisconsin, so we would expect the amount of childhood lead poisoning to differ as well. One question is whether the counties that should be testing the most children for lead poisoning (because they are the counties with the greatest percentage of aging housing) are the counties that are testing the most. A second question is whether the counties that have the highest percentage of children testing positive for lead the ones who really have the greatest number of lead poisoned children, or is it just that those counties screen more of their children?

Data Sources: Wisconsin Environmental Public Health Tracking Program, Census Bureau
